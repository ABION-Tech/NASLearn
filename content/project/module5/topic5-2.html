Module 5: Risk Management

Identifying and Assessing Risks

Step 1: Risk Identification

Risk identification is the first step in the risk management process. It involves recognizing potential threats and opportunities that could affect the project. This process should be ongoing and include input from all project stakeholders.

Methods for Identifying Risks

  • Brainstorming Sessions: Involve team members to discuss potential risks.
  • Expert Judgment: Consulting experienced professionals for insights on possible risks.
  • SWOT Analysis: Identifying project strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.
  • Checklists: Using predefined lists of common project risks.
  • Historical Data Review: Examining past projects to identify recurring risks.

Types of Risks

  • Known Risks: Risks that can be identified and planned for in advance.
  • Unknown Risks: Unforeseen risks that emerge during the project.
  • Internal Risks: Risks within the project’s control, such as resource availability.
  • External Risks: Risks outside the project’s control, such as market conditions or natural disasters.

Step 2: Risk Assessment

After identifying risks, they must be assessed to determine their impact and likelihood. This helps prioritize risks based on their severity and probability.

Qualitative Risk Assessment

This approach categorizes risks based on likelihood and impact, often using a risk matrix.

Likelihood Low Impact Medium Impact High Impact
Low Monitor Monitor Mitigate
Medium Monitor Mitigate Plan Response
High Mitigate Plan Response Immediate Action

Quantitative Risk Assessment

This method assigns numerical values to risks to measure their potential impact.

  • Expected Monetary Value (EMV): Calculates the cost of risks using probability and impact.
  • Decision Tree Analysis: Uses scenarios to determine possible outcomes.
  • Monte Carlo Simulation: Uses computational models to assess risk probabilities.

Step 3: Risk Prioritization

Once risks are assessed, they must be prioritized to ensure the most critical ones are managed first.

  1. High-Priority Risks: Require immediate mitigation plans.
  2. Medium-Priority Risks: Need proactive management and monitoring.
  3. Low-Priority Risks: Should be tracked but may not need immediate action.
