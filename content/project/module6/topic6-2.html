Week 6: Quality, Procurement, and Stakeholder Management

6.2: Project Procurement Management

Introduction: The Art of Strategic Sourcing

Few projects are completed using solely in-house resources. Project Procurement Management is the process of purchasing or acquiring products, services, or results from outside the project team. It's a critical strategic function that, when done correctly, provides access to specialized skills, reduces costs, and accelerates schedules. When done poorly, it introduces significant risk, delays, and budget overruns. This module will equip you with the framework to make smart "make-or-buy" decisions and to manage external partnerships effectively.

1. Make-or-Buy Analysis: The Fundamental Decision

The first step in procurement is deciding if you even should procure. A Make-or-Buy Analysis is a strategic decision-making process used to determine whether a project activity should be performed internally by the organization ("Make") or acquired from an external supplier ("Buy").

Key Factors to Consider
  • Core Competency: Is this activity a core function of our business? Does it provide a competitive advantage? Never outsource your core competency.
    • Example: A top-tier bakery would not outsource the recipe development for its signature pastry.
  • Cost: Which option is more cost-effective? Consider not just the direct cost but also indirect costs like management overhead, training, and potential cost of poor quality.
    • Example: Building a custom internal server might have a high upfront cost but lower long-term operational expenses, while buying cloud services has a low upfront cost but recurring fees.
  • Expertise & Resources: Do we have the necessary skills, knowledge, equipment, and available personnel in-house? If not, how difficult and expensive would it be to develop or acquire them?
    • Example: A small startup building a website might not have a full-time security expert, so they would "buy" a security audit from a consulting firm.
  • Control & Flexibility: How much control do we need over the process, schedule, and intellectual property? "Making" provides more control; "buying" often means less direct oversight.
  • Risk: Who is best positioned to manage the risks associated with this work? Consider the risk of delays, cost overruns, technical failure, and confidentiality.
  • Timeline: Can our internal team complete the work within the required timeframe? External vendors may be able to start and finish faster due to specialized focus and resources.

The Outcome: This analysis results in a formal justification for either using internal resources or proceeding to develop procurement documents (like an RFP) for external vendors.

2. The Procurement Process: A Lifecycle View

Procurement is not a single event but a structured process that runs parallel to the project lifecycle. It is often broken down into four key processes.

1. Plan Procurements

Purpose: To document project procurement decisions, specify the approach, and identify potential sellers.

Key Activities:

  • Create the Procurement Management Plan: How will procurements be managed from initiation through closure?
  • Develop Procurement Documents: Prepare the documents used to solicit proposals, such as a Request for Proposal (RFP), Request for Quotation (RFQ), or Invitation for Bid (IFB).
  • Define Source Selection Criteria: How will you choose a vendor? Common criteria include: price, technical approach, reputation, past performance, and warranty terms.
2. Conduct Procurements

Purpose: To obtain seller responses, select a seller, and award a contract.

Key Activities:

  • Seller Selection: This is often a multi-step process:
    1. Advertise: Distribute the RFP to potential sellers.
    2. Proposal Evaluation: Hold a bidders' conference, receive proposals, and score them against the pre-defined selection criteria.
    3. Select & Negotiate: Choose the best seller and negotiate the final contract terms and pricing.
  • Award a Contract: Finalize and sign the legal agreement.
3. Control Procurements

Purpose: To manage procurement relationships, monitor contract performance, and make changes as necessary.

Key Activities:

  • Performance Review: Ensure the vendor's work meets the agreed-upon milestones, quality standards, and other KPIs outlined in the contract.
  • Payment Systems: Process invoices and make payments according to the contract terms.
  • Change Control: Manage any changes to the procurement statement of work, budget, or timeline through a formal change request process. All changes must be documented in writing.
  • Claims Administration: Handle any disputes or disagreements that arise between the buyer and seller.
4. Close Procurements

Purpose: To complete each procurement formally, including resolution of any open items.

Key Activities:

  • Verification: Confirm that all work described in the contract has been completed satisfying the stakeholders and achieving project deliverables
