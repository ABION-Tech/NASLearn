Prototyping for Interaction

Low-Fidelity vs. High-Fidelity Prototypes

Prototyping is a crucial step in the design process, enabling teams to visualize and test their concepts. Prototypes can be classified into two main categories: low-fidelity and high-fidelity.

Low-Fidelity Prototypes:

  • Definition: Basic, non-interactive representations of a product that focus on the overall structure and layout.
  • Purpose: Used for early-stage testing, concept validation, and gathering feedback on the overall design.
  • Examples: Paper sketches, wireframes, click-through prototypes.

High-Fidelity Prototypes:

  • Definition: Detailed, interactive representations of a product that closely resemble the final design.
  • Purpose: Used for testing specific interactions, evaluating usability, and presenting to stakeholders.
  • Examples: Interactive prototypes created using tools like Figma, Adobe XD, or InVision.

When to Use Each

Choosing between low-fidelity and high-fidelity prototypes depends on the stage of the design process and the specific goals of the testing.

Low-Fidelity Prototypes:

  • Early-Stage Testing: When exploring different concepts and gathering initial feedback.
  • Iterative Design: For quickly testing and iterating on different ideas.
  • Limited Resources: When time or budget is limited.

High-Fidelity Prototypes:

  • Detailed Testing: For evaluating specific interactions and usability issues.
  • Stakeholder Presentation: To present the final design to stakeholders and obtain approval.
  • User Testing: For conducting in-depth usability tests with potential users.
