Core Content

Formulating Strong, Testable Hypotheses

A hypothesis is a testable prediction about how a change will affect user behavior. Like a scientist's prediction before an experiment, a strong hypothesis provides focus and direction for your testing efforts.

Effective hypotheses follow this structure:

If [we make a specific change], then [we will observe a specific outcome], because [of a specific rationale based on user behavior or psychology].

Components of a strong hypothesis:

  1. The Change: A specific, measurable modification to your website or app
  2. The Outcome: A specific, measurable result you expect to see
  3. The Rationale: The reason why you believe this change will produce the outcome
Principles of Statistical Significance and Sample Size

Statistical significance helps determine whether test results are due to the changes you made or simply random chance. Like a courtroom requiring evidence "beyond a reasonable doubt," statistical significance provides confidence that your results are reliable.

Key Statistical Concepts for A/B Testing
  1. Statistical Significance: The probability that the difference between variations is not due to random chance
    • Typically expressed as a percentage (95% significance level is standard)
    • 95% significance means there's only a 5% probability that the observed difference occurred by chance
  2. Confidence Level: How certain you can be that the results are accurate
    • 95% confidence level is the industry standard
    • Higher confidence levels (99%) reduce the risk of false positives but require larger samples
  3. P-value: The probability of obtaining results at least as extreme as the observed results, assuming the null hypothesis is true
    • A p-value less than 0.05 (5%) typically indicates statistical significance
    • Lower p-values indicate stronger evidence against the null hypothesis
  4. Sample Size: The number of visitors included in your test
    • Larger sample sizes provide more reliable results
    • Required sample size depends on baseline conversion rate, minimum detectable effect, significance level, and statistical power
  5. Statistical Power: The probability of detecting an effect when there actually is one
    • 80% power is standard (meaning there's a 20% chance of missing a real effect)
    • Higher power requires larger sample sizes
Designing an A/B Test: Variables, Control vs. Variation, and Audience Targeting

Well-designed A/B tests follow a structured methodology that ensures reliable results. Like a scientific experiment controlling variables, proper test design isolates changes to accurately measure their impact.

Key elements of A/B test design:

  1. Variables: What you're testing
    • Independent Variable: The element you change (e.g., button color, headline, image)
    • Dependent Variable: The metric you measure (e.g., click-through rate, conversion rate)
    • Controlled Variables: Elements that remain constant across variations
  2. Control vs. Variation:
    • Control: The original version (often called "Version A")
    • Variation: The modified version (often called "Version B")
    • Some tests include multiple variations (A/B/n tests)
  3. Randomization: Ensuring each visitor has an equal chance of seeing any variation
  4. Traffic Allocation: How much traffic is directed to each variation
  5. Audience Targeting: Determining which visitors are included in the test
  6. Test Duration: How long the test runs
Overview of A/B Testing Tools

A/B testing tools provide the technical infrastructure to design, run, and analyze experiments. Like different types of scientific equipment, each tool has unique strengths and is suited for specific testing scenarios.

In-Lesson Activity

Hypothesis Formulation Exercise
You're working on optimizing the checkout process for an online clothing retailer. Based on user research and analytics data, you've identified several potential issues:

  1. Analytics show that 35% of users who reach the shipping information page abandon the checkout process
  2. Session recordings reveal that users seem confused by the "Create Account" option during checkout
  3. Heatmaps indicate that users are clicking on product images in the cart, expecting to see more details
  4. Customer surveys mention that shipping costs are a primary reason for cart abandonment
Your task is to:
  1. Formulate 3 strong, testable hypotheses based on these insights
  2. For each hypothesis, identify the independent variable, dependent variable, and expected outcome
  3. For one of your hypotheses, outline how you would design an A/B test to validate it
  4. Estimate how long you would need to run the test, assuming the site gets 5,000 checkout visits per week
Take 15 minutes to complete this exercise, then be prepared to share your hypotheses and test design with the class.
